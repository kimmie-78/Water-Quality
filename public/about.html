Water Quality Information

Whether you should have your water tested depends on several factors, particularly those related to your health and your family’s well-being. Water quality issues can manifest as illness or less severe problems like unpleasant taste, color, odor, or staining of clothes and fixtures. Key considerations include the proximity of your water well to septic systems and the materials used in your home’s plumbing, which may affect water safety.

Collecting a water sample for testing requires careful handling to ensure accurate results. Follow these steps:

Equipment Needed

  • Clean, sterilized sample bottle (preferably glass or plastic with a secure lid)
  • Permanent marker for labeling
  • Disposable gloves (optional but recommended)
  • Cooler with ice packs (if transporting to a lab)

Collection Steps

  1. **Prepare the Bottle:** Make sure the sample bottle is clean and sterilized. Rinse it with the water source you will be testing.
  2. **Turn on the Tap:** Let the water run for about 5-10 minutes to flush out any stagnant water in the pipes.
  3. **Collect the Sample:** Fill the bottle to the neck (leaving some air space) and cap it securely.
  4. **Label the Bottle:** Use a permanent marker to label the bottle with the date, time, and location of the sample collection.
  5. **Transport the Sample:** If not testing immediately, store the sample in a cooler with ice packs to maintain its temperature.

How to Check Contaminant Level

The WaterQuality web app provides an interactive way to monitor water quality, input new sample data, analyze contaminants, and visualize trends. By following these steps, users can effectively test and manage water contamination levels to ensure safety and compliance.

Steps to Test Water Contaminants

  1. Access the Dashboard: Navigate to the Water Quality Dashboard via the main link.
  2. View Current Data: Upon loading the page, check the Water Quality Overview section for alerts and the latest sample data.
  3. Add New Sample: Click the Add New Sample button to open the modal. Fill out the form with the contaminant values (like aluminium, arsenic, etc.). Click Submit to save the new sample data. This will help in monitoring water quality over time.
  4. Show History: Click the Show History button to view a list of all previous samples. This popup will display the sample ID and contaminant values, enabling you to track changes over time.
  5. Analyze Contaminant Data: Review the Contaminant Information section to understand which contaminant is currently the highest and its implications for health. This section also provides solutions on how to reduce contaminant levels.
  6. Visualize Data with Charts: Use the line chart to visualize trends in contaminant levels. This helps in identifying periods of concern and can guide your responses. You can toggle the visibility of the line graph by clicking the Show Line Graph button.
  7. Download Data: If you want to keep a record of your data, click the Download CSV button to download all sample data for external analysis.
  8. Refresh Data: If you need the latest data from your source (e.g., if data is being updated in real-time), click the Refresh Data button in the relevant section.

Water analysis methods are crucial for a wide range of industries that rely on water for their operations, like the food and beverage industry. In this industry, water is used extensively in daily operations, such as cleaning, cooking, and ingredient preparation. Therefore, it is essential to analyze the quality of water at different points along the process to ensure that it meets the required standards for consumption and production. By completing a water analysis, you can detect any contaminants or impurities that may affect the taste, safety, and overall quality of the final product(s).

1. Aluminium

Description: Aluminium is a naturally occurring element, but high levels can enter water sources from industrial waste.

Importance: Prolonged exposure to aluminium in drinking water can affect kidney function.

Fix: Aluminium can be removed through chemical coagulation, where the contaminant binds to chemicals added to water, forming larger particles that can be filtered out.

2. Ammonia

Description: Ammonia is commonly found in fertilizers and wastewater.

Importance: High levels can be toxic to aquatic life and indicate pollution.

Fix: It can be removed using biological treatment processes or reverse osmosis systems.

3. Arsenic

Description: A toxic element that can enter water sources from natural deposits or industrial activities.

Importance: Long-term exposure to arsenic is linked to cancer and skin lesions.

Fix: Effective methods include adsorption, reverse osmosis, and distillation.

4. Bacteria

Description: Pathogenic bacteria can enter water sources through sewage and wastewater discharges.

Importance: Exposure can cause gastrointestinal illnesses.

Fix: Bacteria can be eliminated through boiling, chlorination, or UV treatment.

5. Cadmium

Description: Cadmium is a heavy metal that can enter water through industrial discharges and corrosion of pipes.

Importance: Exposure to cadmium can lead to kidney damage and bone loss.

Fix: Cadmium can be removed through reverse osmosis and ion exchange methods.

6. Chlorine

Description: A chemical used in water treatment to kill harmful microorganisms.

Importance: While beneficial, high chlorine levels can cause taste and odor issues.

Fix: Activated carbon filters can remove excess chlorine from water.

7. Chromium

Description: Chromium can enter water from industrial processes and is a known carcinogen.

Importance: Exposure can lead to serious health issues, including cancer.

Fix: Chromium can be treated with reduction methods or through reverse osmosis.

8. Copper

Description: Copper can leach into water from plumbing systems and is a common contaminant.

Importance: Excessive copper can cause gastrointestinal issues.

Fix: Copper levels can be reduced using reverse osmosis and activated carbon filtration.

9. Fluoride

Description: Fluoride is often added to drinking water for dental health but can be harmful in excess.

Importance: High levels can lead to dental and skeletal fluorosis.

Fix: Fluoride can be removed using reverse osmosis systems.

10. Bacteria

Description: Bacteria can contaminate water from sewage and agricultural runoff.

Importance: Pathogenic bacteria can cause serious illness and infections.

Fix: Water can be treated using UV light or chlorination to kill bacteria.

11. Viruses

Description: Viruses can enter water supplies through human or animal waste.

Importance: They can cause waterborne diseases, posing serious health risks.

Fix: Effective filtration and UV treatment can eliminate viruses from water.

12. Lead

Description: Lead can leach into drinking water from old pipes and plumbing fixtures.

Importance: Lead exposure can have serious health impacts, especially in children.

Fix: Lead can be reduced through point-of-use filters or by replacing lead-containing pipes.

13. Nitrates

Description: Nitrates often enter water from fertilizers and sewage.

Importance: High levels can cause methemoglobinemia, or "blue baby syndrome," in infants.

Fix: Nitrates can be removed using ion exchange or reverse osmosis.

14. Nitrites

Description: Nitrites can form from the breakdown of nitrates and are harmful in high concentrations.

Importance: They can affect oxygen transport in the blood.

Fix: Similar to nitrates, nitrites can be removed through ion exchange methods.

15. Mercury

Description: Mercury can enter water through industrial discharges and mining activities.

Importance: It can accumulate in fish and pose serious health risks to humans.

Fix: Mercury can be treated using activated carbon filtration and distillation methods.

16. Perchlorate

Description: Perchlorate is a chemical used in rocket fuel and can contaminate water supplies.

Importance: It can interfere with thyroid function.

Fix: Perchlorate can be removed through ion exchange and reverse osmosis.

17. Radium

Description: Radium is a naturally occurring radioactive element that can contaminate water sources.

Importance: Long-term exposure can increase the risk of cancer.

Fix: Radium can be removed using reverse osmosis and ion exchange methods.

18. Selenium

Description: Selenium can enter water from industrial processes and natural deposits.

Importance: High levels can lead to reproductive and developmental problems.

Fix: Selenium can be treated using biological treatment and filtration methods.

19. Silver

Description: Silver can enter water through industrial waste and can have antimicrobial properties.

Importance: While low levels are not harmful, high concentrations can be toxic.

Fix: Silver can be removed using activated carbon filtration.

20. Uranium

Description: Uranium can contaminate water from natural deposits and mining activities.

Importance: Prolonged exposure can lead to kidney damage and increased cancer risk.

Fix: Uranium can be removed through reverse osmosis and ion exchange methods.
